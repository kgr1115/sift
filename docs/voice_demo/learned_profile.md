# Voice profile — learned from Kyle's sent mail

Generated by `sift learn-voice --limit 30` on 2026-04-21. The learner fetches
the last N messages from the user's Gmail Sent folder and runs a single
Claude call to compress them into (a) a prose style guide the drafter can
follow and (b) three verbatim example replies used as in-context anchors.

Sample size: **23 sent messages** (30 message stubs returned, 7 filtered out
as empty/forwards during body extraction).

## Summary

Kyle writes in a consistently warm, polished-but-approachable register across
almost all contexts — there is very little tonal gap between his personal and
professional emails. The biggest differentiator is salutation and sign-off
formality, not vocabulary or sentence case.

**Register rules:** Kyle always uses sentence case and full punctuation
regardless of recipient. He does not write in lowercase informally.
Professional/institutional emails open with "Hello [Name]," or "Hello,"
(generic); close with "Thank you," + "Kyle Rauch" (full name). Casual forwards
or one-liners to close contacts often have no body text at all — just the
forwarded content and his name block.

**Openers:** "Hello [First Name]!" with an exclamation point is his default
warm opener. "Hello," (no name, no exclamation) is used for anonymous/support
contacts. He never uses "Hi," "Dear," or "Hey" — the sole exception is "Dear
Customer Support Team" in a cold service email, which reads as consciously
more formal.

**Closers:** "Thank you," followed by a line break and "Kyle Rauch" is his
standard. For warmer or more enthusiastic closings he uses "Thank you so much!"
or "Thanks!" before his name. He almost never uses "Best," "Regards," or
"Sincerely."

**Length:** Short to medium — typically 2–5 sentences. He states his request
or reply clearly, adds a polite framing line (appreciation or anticipation),
and closes. He does not write multi-paragraph emails. No bullet points,
numbered lists, or bold text in body copy.

**Vocabulary tells:** Heavy use of exclamation points to signal warmth ("Thank
you so much!", "I really appreciate it!", "looking forward to speaking with
you!"). Phrases he reaches for: "I really appreciate it," "looking forward
to," "much appreciated," "I do look forward to." No slang, no abbreviations
(no "lmk," "btw," etc.), no em-dashes, no parentheticals.

**Quirks:** Kyle frequently sends bare-forward or bare-link emails with zero
body text — just his name block. This is a notable habit for sharing info
with close contacts.

## What's interesting about this output

The hand-written `DEFAULT_VOICE` in `src/sift/voice.py` guessed "comfortable
with lowercase for casual threads" and "signs personal emails with just
'Kyle'". Both are **wrong** for the real corpus — the learner caught it. The
hand-written fallback generalized from "software-engineer sending casual Slack
messages" while the real sent-mail corpus skews toward job-search outreach,
recruiter replies, and customer-support threads where Kyle consistently uses
sentence case and "Kyle Rauch".

That correction — from a plausible-but-wrong a priori guess to an
evidence-grounded profile — is exactly what the voice-learning feature is
supposed to do. If the drafter had been running on `DEFAULT_VOICE`, it would
have produced lower-case drafts signed "Kyle" for every outreach, which is
subtly but noticeably off.
